How Do I Know My Ex Isn't Drinking Before Picking Up Our Daughter?

One mother's search for peace of mind led to a solution neither parent expected to embrace

Published: February 7, 2026

Every custody exchange shouldn't feel like a gamble with your child's safety

Every other Friday at 6 PM, Sarah watches from her kitchen window as her ex-husband pulls into the driveway to pick up their seven-year-old daughter, Emma, for the weekend.

She watches for the signs. The slight hesitation before he opens the car door. The way he fumbles with his keys. The too-cheerful greeting that doesn't quite reach his eyes.

"I spent months with this knot in my stomach," Sarah says, her voice quiet. "But what could I do? Accuse him without proof? That would make me look vindictive in court. Say nothing and send Emma anyway? That felt like I was failing to protect her."

It's a dilemma thousands of divorced parents face silently, unable to talk about it with friends, family, or even their lawyers. The fear is real. The proof is nowhere. And your child is caught in the middle.

The Impossible Position

  • You can't prove they're drinking, but your gut tells you something is wrong
  • Bringing it up creates conflict and makes you look like the "difficult" parent in court
  • Your child starts asking "Why are you and dad always fighting?"
  • You lie awake wondering if this is the weekend something terrible happens
  • Other parents judge you: "Just let it go" or "You're being overprotective"
  • Your lawyer says "without evidence, there's nothing we can do"

Sarah's breaking point came on a school night. Emma came home from her dad's weekend visibly shaken. When Sarah asked what was wrong, Emma said quietly: "Daddy was acting weird. He kept falling asleep on the couch. I tried to wake him up for dinner but he just yelled at me."

That's when Sarah knew she couldn't stay silent anymore. But she also knew that without proof, any accusation would backfire. She'd been down that road before‚Äîconfronting him, arguing, being told she was "paranoid" and "controlling."

The family court mediator had made it clear: allegations without evidence would only hurt her case.

"What if we both agreed to verification? What if we took the guessing out of it entirely?"

Late one night, after another restless Friday evening, Sarah had an idea that seemed almost too simple: What if both parents agreed to prove they were sober before custody exchanges?

Not as an accusation. Not as a punishment. But as a commitment to Emma's safety that they both could make.

She was nervous bringing it up. But the next day, she sent her ex a text: "I've been thinking about Emma's safety during transitions. What if we both used a monitoring system so there's never any doubt? For her peace of mind, and ours?"

She expected resistance. She expected an argument. Instead, his response came quickly: "I've been wanting to prove to you I'm doing better. Let's do it."

Yes. BACtrack View provides court-admissible data logs including GPS location, time stamps, facial verification photos, and BAC results. Many family law attorneys submit this data during custody hearings.

You'll receive an alert that the test was missed. This is documented in the system. Repeated missed tests can be presented as evidence of non-compliance if there's a court order for monitoring.
